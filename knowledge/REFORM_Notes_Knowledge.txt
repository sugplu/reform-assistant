[NOTE 9] Transparency Act ?
----------------------------------------
Transparency Act ?

For a better California committee

The �Transparency Act� mailer you received is referring to the Audit Special Taxes Initiative.

This initiative is designed to make it harder for politicians to impose new special taxes and adds a transparency requirement by mandating audits of programs that receive special tax revenues. The goal is to ensure the money from those taxes is actually being spent the way voters were originally promised and not diverted elsewhere.

Reform California is helping collect signatures for this petition, along with the Stop the Savings Tax Initiative, as part of the effort to qualify both measures for the 2026 ballot.

[NOTE 28] Mail Tray Info
----------------------------------------
Mail Tray Info

These mail trays are part of our voter outreach effort. They allow volunteers to help prepare large batches of voter guide mailers either from home or at one of our offices.

Each tray contains 500 letters, 500 envelopes, return address labels, and simple instructions. Your role is to hand address the envelopes and insert the prepared voter letter into each one. Hand-addressed mail significantly increases the likelihood that voters will actually open and read it.

You do not pay for postage and you do not mail them yourself. Once you complete the tray, you simply return it to the office, and our team handles the final processing and mailing.

You can pick up a tray and work at your own pace from home, or complete them in-office during walk-in hours. Our offices are open Monday through Friday�at:

[NOTE 53] ADU in community:

ADU in community:

California state laws passed since 2019, such as SB 9 and SB 10, have significantly reduced local control over housing developments. These laws eliminate single-family zoning and allow developers to build multiple units on parcels previously zoned for one home, often without local approval. Additionally, the �Builders Remedy� provision lets developers bypass local city council approvals for certain projects.

If the project is government-subsidized and rent-controlled, it may escape even more local oversight. Unfortunately, local city councils often have limited power to stop these developments due to these state laws.

However, there is still one key protection: Article XXXIV of the California Constitution, which requires voter approval for publicly funded affordable housing projects. But this protection is under threat from SCA-2, a proposed constitutional amendment that would eliminate this voter approval requirement.

What you can do:

- Get involved with Reform California�s campaign to restore local control by opposing laws like SB 9, SB 10, and SCA-2. This includes signing petitions and contributing to efforts that fight for community input on housing projects.

- Attend local city council meetings to voice your concerns and organize with neighbors to push for projects that fit the character of your community.

- Support reform-minded candidates who prioritize local control and sensible housing policies.

Ink outside signature box

However, California election officials do review signatures carefully to verify voter identity, and significant discrepancies or issues with the signature could lead to a ballot being rejected.

[NOTE 159] Cure ballot
----------------------------------------
Cure ballot 

Ballot curing is the process of a ballot not getting counted because of an error such as a mismatching signature (which can be an honest mistake), and then the voter has an opportunity to affirm that they intended to vote and want their vote counted. They get to &quotcure" their rejected ballot.

Campaigns are able to get a list of voters whose ballots were rejected to help reach out to them and urge them to &quotcure" their ballot and make sure it gets counted.

In order to &quotcure" a ballot, the registered voter has to confirm they intended to and did vote � which means any votes by people who don't exist don't get cured and counted.
